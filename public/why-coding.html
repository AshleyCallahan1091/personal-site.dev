Why Coding?
June 29.2016






Why coding? It's a popular question among people when they ask what I go to school for. Why software development? The short answer?

Why not?


Let's start by painting a picture. Nine months ago, my husband went off to training for an entire month. I asked him if there was anything he needed me to do while he was gone and he said "Get a job." He was only joking, of course. My husband is not a third derivative (weak calculus joke) and I knew he was only joking. That month of training did not allow for overtime so money would be a little tight but it wasn't a necessity for me to start working again. Even still, I went out and got a job that day.


It wasn't a great job; I handed out samples of frozen yogurt for 3-7 hours a day and fielded questions like "I'm on a diet and need something sugar-free and fat-free with the smallest amount of calories so what are my options?" I stayed there for six months, making just above minimum wage and ended up working a couple different side jobs to bring in more income when my husband and I moved into our own apartment.


After a particularly frustrating day between cleaning houses and being on my feet all day, I passed the "Learn to Code - Codeup" billboard for the thirtieth time and thought to myself, "I can see myself learning to code. What else have I got to lose?"


I took a web development class in high school and tinkered with HTML, Photoshop and Dreamweaver and it was pretty fun. I didn't even think to pursue it in college because it was almost too much fun and high school teaches you pretty early on that your career isn't supposed to be fun if you want to actually earn money. Of course, this is coming from someone who spent two years as a Liberal Arts major in college and look how well that worked out for me - serving FroYo samples for $7.75/hr, surrounded by high school kids.


So, in all honesty, I really had nothing left to lose.


I sent in a request for more information and within the next couple days, I was on the phone with Dylan, asking any questions I could think of while I was on my way to work. I submitted my application that week and less than a month later, I found myself in the Codeup classroom learning a new set of skills.


But why Codeup? Why not a computer science degree?


Well, why would I spend four years learning a set of skills I can knock out in four months for a fraction of the cost without the hassle of schedules and classes I don't even need specifically for coding just to maintain full-time student status? I have nothing against college. I have some awesome memories from Northeast Lakeview. I learned some very interesting skills but the only real thing I have to show for it is my charcoal self-portrait on my homepage. What Codeup offers is the potential for a full-time career you'll love at the end of the program, not the necessity for a full-time job in any field just to pay off an enormous amount of student loan debt. Four months is a perfect amount of time because there isn't time to get burned out. You don't have to drag yourself out of bed for four years, wondering if you even want to stick with your degree.


Isn't four months an insane amount of time to learn such a large quantity of material and topics? Absolutely. Is it doable? You bet. Will it be hard? Yes. Especially so if you have a family. There will be missed family dinners, less playtime, less dates and more late nights than not. But it's only four months. If you can sacrifice sixteen weeks, you'll get back a lifetime of opportunities to support your family while doing something you're passionate about and live the life you want because you had the guts to tackle learning a skill set in a bootcamp instead of the path of traditional education.


In my aforementioned moment of frustration that kickstarted this experience, I discovered that the only thing stopping me from creating a vibrant career was me.


Now for my last question.


What's stopping you?

-Ashley
